1. What is Artificial Intelligence?

At its core, AI is about creating computer systems that can perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. Think of it as teaching computers to learn from experience, recognize patterns, and make decisions.

Key Insight: Most modern AI you use (like ChatGPT, Claude, or image generators) falls under "Generative AI" - they create new content rather than just classifying or predicting existing data.

2. How Machine Learning Works

Traditional programming is like giving someone a recipe: "If you see X, do Y." Machine learning is different - you show the computer thousands of examples and let it figure out the patterns on its own.

The Three Types of Machine Learning

1. Supervised Learning

Learning with labeled examples

  • You provide questions AND answers
  • Like a student with an answer key
  • Example: Email spam detection, image recognition

2. Unsupervised Learning

Finding patterns without labels

  • You provide data, AI finds patterns
  • Like exploring without a map
  • Example: Customer segmentation, anomaly detection

3. Reinforcement Learning

Learning through trial and error

  • AI tries actions and gets rewards/penalties
  • Like training a dog with treats
  • Example: Game-playing AIs, robotics, self-driving cars

How They Work: A Simple Example

Let's say you want to identify if a photo contains a cat:

1. Input Layer: The image pixels go in (like colors and brightness values)
2. Hidden Layers: Early layers detect simple features (edges, colors), later layers detect complex features (whiskers, ears, cat faces)
3. Output Layer: Final answer: "Yes, this is a cat" or "No, it's not"
Deep Learning: When you have many hidden layers (sometimes hundreds!), it's called "deep learning." More layers = ability to learn more complex patterns.

4. Training: How AI Actually Learns

Training an AI model is like teaching someone to ride a bike - they'll fall a lot at first, but they get better with practice and feedback.

Key Training Concepts

Parameters/Weights: These are the "knobs" the AI adjusts during training. A model with "175 billion parameters" has 175 billion adjustable numbers that get fine-tuned to learn patterns. More parameters generally = more capable, but also more expensive to train and run.
Training Data: The examples you show the AI. For language models like me, this is billions of words from books, websites, articles, and more. The quality and diversity of this data is crucial.
Epochs: One complete pass through all the training data. Models typically train for many epochs, seeing the same data multiple times to really learn the patterns.

The "Attention" Mechanism: This is the breakthrough that makes LLMs work so well. It allows the model to focus on relevant parts of the input when generating each word. For example, when writing "The cat sat on the ___", it pays attention to "cat" to know "mat" is more likely than "roof."

How I Generate Responses

When you ask me a question, here's what happens behind the scenes:

  1. I don't search a database - I don't look up answers. Instead, I predict the most likely next word, then the next, then the next, based on patterns I learned during training.
  2. I generate one token at a time - Each word (or piece of a word) is generated sequentially based on what came before.
  3. I use probability - For each position, I'm calculating "given everything so far, what's the most appropriate next token?" There's usually some randomness too, so I don't always give the exact same answer.
  4. I have a context window - I can only "remember" a certain amount of our conversation (for me, it's about 200,000 tokens or ~150,000 words).

6. Important Concepts to Understand

Training vs Inference

Training (Learning Phase)

  • Happens once (or occasionally for updates)
  • Requires massive compute power
  • Takes weeks or months
  • Costs millions of dollars
  • Uses specialized hardware (GPUs, TPUs)

Inference (Using Phase)

  • Happens every time you use the AI
  • Much less compute needed
  • Takes seconds
  • Relatively cheap per query
  • What's happening when you chat with me

Context Window & Memory

Context Window: The amount of text an LLM can "see" at once. Think of it as short-term memory. For older models, this was ~4,000 tokens (~3,000 words). Modern models like Claude can handle 200,000+ tokens (~150,000 words).

Important: LLMs don't have true memory between conversations (unless specifically designed with it). Each conversation is independent, and I can only "remember" what's in our current conversation window.

Temperature & Creativity

Setting What It Does Best For
Low Temperature (0-0.3) More deterministic, picks the most likely words Factual answers, code, math, consistency
Medium Temperature (0.4-0.7) Balanced between predictable and creative General conversation, writing assistance
High Temperature (0.8-1.0) More random, picks less likely words Creative writing, brainstorming, diverse ideas

Fine-Tuning & RLHF

Fine-Tuning: After initial training on general text, models are further trained on specific data to specialize them. For example, medical AI might be fine-tuned on medical journals.

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback): Humans rate AI responses as good or bad, and the model learns to give responses more like the highly-rated ones. This is how models learn to be helpful, harmless, and honest.

8. Important Limitations to Understand

‚ùå Hallucinations

AI models can confidently state false information. They're pattern-matching machines, not fact databases. They might "fill in the blanks" with plausible-sounding but incorrect information.

What to do: Verify important facts, especially for medical, legal, or financial advice.

üìÖ Knowledge Cutoff

Models are trained on data up to a certain date. I was trained on data through January 2025, so I don't know about events after that unless I search the web.

üéØ Context Understanding

While very good, AI can still miss subtle context, sarcasm, or cultural nuances that humans would easily catch.

üîí No True Reasoning

Despite appearances, current AI doesn't "understand" in the way humans do. It's incredibly sophisticated pattern matching, but not conscious thought.

9. Where AI is Heading

Current Trends

Larger Context Windows: Models that can "remember" entire books or conversations
Multimodal Integration: Seamlessly handling text, images, audio, and video together
Specialized Models: AI fine-tuned for specific industries (medical, legal, engineering)
Agentic AI: AI that can use tools, plan multi-step tasks, and operate semi-autonomously
Smaller, Efficient Models: Powerful AI that runs on your phone or laptop
Better Reasoning: Models that can break down complex problems step-by-step
